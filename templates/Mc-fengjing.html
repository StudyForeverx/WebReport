Ruins of St. Paul's
The predecessor of the Ruins of St. Paul's is St. Paul's Church, which is an affiliated part of St. Paul's College founded by the Jesuits in Macau. In Catholicism, St. Paul is described as one of the greatest missionaries. He went out for missionary activities three times and traveled to places such as the Mediterranean and the Italian Peninsula. The college is named after him, which means that the Jesuits went to the East to preach. Carrying on the work of St. Paul[8]. The Chinese name "Samba" comes from the Chinese transliteration of the Portuguese "São Paulo" of St. Paul; in addition, in order to distinguish it from the church of St. Joseph's Seminary, a branch of St. Paul's College, it is also named after the former. Its scale is larger than the latter, so the Chinese call St. Paul's Church "Big St. Paul's" and St. Joseph's Seminary "Little St. Paul's" or "Samba Tsai". In 1835, a fire destroyed St. Paul's College and the church. The front wall of the church, most of the foundation, and the 68 stone steps in front of the church remain. Because the locals believed that the front wall of the church resembled a traditional Chinese archway, they were removed. Known as the "Ruins of St. Paul's"

Macau Fisherman's Wharf
It is Macau's first themed cultural, creative, entertainment and tourism complex, equipped with a shopping mall modeled after Fisherman's Wharf in Europe and the United States. Macau Fisherman's Wharf was built opposite the new reclamation area of the outer harbor and adjacent to the new Hong Kong and Macau Ferry Terminal. It was invested and built by Stanley Ho and Chow Kam Fai with a total investment of approximately MOP 1.85 billion. It took 5 years of preparation and construction. The opening ceremony and trial operation were held on December 31, 2005 by Macau Chief Executive Edmund Ho Hau Wah and Fisherman's Wharf Chairman Stanley Ho. It was officially opened on December 23, 2006 after one year of trial operation. It was formerly the old Macau Hong Kong and Macau Ferry Terminal.

Guan-ye Street, Macau
Located in the center of Taipa, Macau; 150 meters long and 5 meters wide.It intersects with Rua Direita Carlos Eugénio and Rua Correia da Silva in a T shape to the south, and forms a T shape with Rua do Regedor and the fire station in the north. Macau Ma-zu Pavilion
Mazu Pavilion (Portuguese: Templo de A-Má), commonly known as A-Má Temple (Cantonese Pinyin: ma5 gok3 miu6, "Ma" is changed to "horse" , Portuguese: Pagode da Barra), formerly known as the Empress Temple , Haijue Temple and Tianfei Temple are a temple located on the hillside of A-Ma Mountain in the southwest of the Macau Peninsula that mainly worships the sea god Mazu. It was founded about 1553 to 1557 years ago and is one of the landmark buildings in Macau. , together with Lin Feng Temple and Puji Temple, are the three major ancient temples in Macau, and it is also the oldest temple among them.

Longhuan Puyun Residential Museum (Portuguese: Casas-Museu da Taipa), referred to as Longhuan Puyun in Chinese. Located on the seaside road in Taipa, Macau. It is a museum based on five Portuguese-style houses on the seaside road. It is also one of Macau's popular tourist attractions
Shimen National Forest Park
Shimen National Forest Park is located in the northern part of Guangzhou and is celebrated for its pristine natural landscapes and rich biodiversity. The park covers a vast area, offering a peaceful retreat with lush forests, clear streams, and scenic hiking trails. It is particularly famous for its picturesque waterfalls and the Shimen Reservoir, which is the largest man-made lake in Guangzhou. Visitors to the park can engage in activities such as hiking, bird-watching, and picnicking, making it an ideal destination for nature lovers and families looking for an escape into the wilderness.
Coloane
Coloane (Portuguese: Coloane) was formerly known as Yan Bay, Yan Zao Bay, Jiu'ao Island or Apowei. It was named after Jiu'ao Bay in the northeast corner and Jiu'ao Mountain on the island. In addition, some people believe that the Portuguese name of Coloane is a transliteration of the island's ancient Cantonese name "Kuaoluwan". It is now a place in Macau and one of the four major parts (regions) that make up Macau. Since most of the island has not yet been developed, with rich vegetation and beautiful scenery, Coloane is known as Macau's last back garden. The area above the halfway point of Dieshitang Mountain, the highest peak on Coloane Island, is designated as a country park and Mazu Cultural Village and is strictly protected.
Taipa Grande
Taipa Grande (Portuguese: Taipa Grande), also known as Taipa, Taipa Mountain and Chicken Neck Mountain. Located in Taipa, Macau, with an altitude of 160.4 meters, it is the highest point of Taipa Island.
